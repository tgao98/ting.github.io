What impact do you want to have on the world and why?

As cheesy as it sounds, I want to leave the world a better place than I found it. Specifically, I want to develop and design new and better technology to not only improve daily life, but also to make it more accessible and meet the demands of a growing, changing world.

For two decades, my grandmother was nearly deaf due to an ear infection. Hearing aids existed at the time, but they were too expensive in China (and everywhere else in the world). She forgot the sound of her children’s voices, she had trouble crossing the street, she could not tune in to her favorite radio station, and so much more. When my family moved to America and my grandmother was able to obtain a hearing aid, she stayed up for three days straight, listening to opera music, because she was convinced she was in a dream and didn’t want to wake up. Her experience had a profound impact on me, because I witnessed firsthand the phenomenon that is technology -- not just its power to change lives, but its relentless pursuit to becoming better, smarter, cheaper.

For perspective: In 1983, a cell phone cost about $4,000, and only a handful of people had access to one. Now, cell phones are ubiquitous, with flip phones as low as $4 and smart phones starting at $300.

New technology is interesting, but efficient and accessible technology is profound -- and I want to better the world by elevating technology to a level of “profoundness.”

What is the latest product you’re absolutely obsessed with and why?

Facebook Messenger. Whether it’s forming a spontaneous group chat for a trip to New York, planning a friend’s birthday party, or discussing group project details, my go-to is Messenger. The ease of direct messaging people, finding contacts from Facebook profiles, creating group chats, and stimulating engagement with gifs and stickers makes Messenger a perfect tool for students like me. A large part of Messenger’s success comes from its intimate connection with Facebook; it’s no wonder that with 2.7 billion Facebook users, there are 1.3 billion active Messenger users sending 8 billion messages per month. Another draw of Messenger, besides its access to Facebook contacts, is how intuitive it is to use and how, unlike its competitors, it’s media content of viral videos, gifs, memes, and stickers is constantly and rapidly expanding.

Messenger is still missing a key ingredient, though: in-app scheduling. When arranging meeting times or meals with friends in Messenger, it is difficult to keep up with the various times that people propose in the chat, and there is currently no way to link an appointment time directly to a calendar after it’s been confirmed in the chat. A straightforward way of dealing with this is to recognize dates within messages and be able to link them to a person’s choice calendar, much like what Gmail does with Gcal. Facebook could also implement this feature with Facebook events to further lessen scheduling hassles.
